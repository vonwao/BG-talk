# The Man Who Said You Can Receive Help
*The message of Bruno Gröning*

**Speaker script — DRAFT v1**
**Target: ~10 minutes**

---

## 1. Opening Hook (~1.5 min)

Imagine you're sick. You've been sick for a long time. You've tried everything — doctors, treatments, advice. Nothing has worked.

And then someone tells you: "There's a man. He doesn't prescribe anything. He doesn't operate. He just says — you can be helped."

You'd probably say, "That's ridiculous."

But in 1949, in a small town in northern Germany called Herford, thousands of people showed up to hear exactly that. Thousands. Not because they were gullible. Because they were desperate. And because something was happening around this man that no one could easily explain.

His name was Bruno Gröning.

I want to tell you about his message tonight — not because I think you should believe it, but because I found something in it that I think is worth hearing.

---

## 2. The Modern Problem (~1.5 min)

We live in a world that treats the human being as a machine.

Something breaks — you fix it. You go to a specialist. They look at the part that's failing. They intervene mechanically or chemically. And often that works, and thank God for that.

But most of us, at some point, have had the quiet feeling that something is missing from this picture. That we are more than the sum of our parts. That there's a dimension of the human experience that doesn't show up on a scan.

And yet — if you say that out loud in most rooms, people get uncomfortable. Because we've been trained to believe that if you can't measure it, it isn't real.

So here's the question I want to sit with tonight: What if help is available to us from a source we've learned to ignore?

---

## 3. Bruno Gröning — Who Was He? (~2.5 min)

Germany, 1949. The war is over. The country is in ruins — physically, spiritually, psychologically. People are suffering in ways that go far beyond what medicine can reach.

And in the middle of this, a man appears. He's not a doctor. He's not a priest. He has no institution behind him. He's a simple man — born in 1906 in Danzig — and he begins to say something that sounds almost too simple:

"Help is available. You can receive it. But you have to do something first — you have to turn away from what is weighing you down, and open yourself to the good."

He called this force the *Heilstrom* — which translates roughly as "healing stream." Think of it as a current of goodness, a spiritual energy that is always present but that most people have cut themselves off from.

His teaching wasn't complicated. It came down to a few things:

Stop clinging to what's wrong. Don't make your illness or your problem the center of your identity.

Turn toward what is good. Not as wishful thinking — but as an inner act of trust.

And then — receive. Not through effort or willpower, but through openness. Through a kind of inner stillness that allows something to flow in.

People reported extraordinary things. Healings that had no medical explanation. Changes that seemed to come from the inside out. And these weren't just stories — the community that carries on his work today, the Circle of Friends of Bruno Gröning, has a medical-scientific group that documents these reports with the rigor of clinical case studies.

Now — I'm not standing here to tell you that's proof. But I think it's worth paying attention to.

---

## 4. Echoes of Older Traditions (~1.5 min)

When I first encountered Bruno Gröning's teaching, what struck me was how familiar it felt.

Not because I'd heard of him before — I hadn't. But because so much of what he said reminded me of things I'd encountered in much older traditions.

Jesus said, "The kingdom of God is within you." He told people, "Your faith has made you well." He spoke of living water — a spiritual force that flows through those who are open to it.

And if you listen carefully, the pattern is the same:

There is a source of good that is greater than us. Human beings can connect to it. But it requires something — an inner turning. A letting go. A trust.

I'm not saying Bruno Gröning was the same as Jesus. That's not the point. The point is that this idea keeps appearing — across centuries, across cultures, through different people in different times.

What Gröning seemed to do was express it again in language that felt immediate and alive for modern people. Not theology. Not doctrine. Just: turn away from the bad, open yourself to the good, and let the healing stream in.

---

## 5. Personal Reflection (~1.5 min)

I should tell you why I'm up here talking about this.

I'm a software engineer. I've spent 25 years building technology — and more recently, building AI systems. That's my professional world. Logic, systems, code.

But for years, I've also been drawn to a different kind of question. The question of what the human being actually is. Whether there's more to us than what fits in a materialist framework.

And when I encountered the teaching of Bruno Gröning, something in it rang true. Not because someone told me to believe it. But because parts of it matched what I had already sensed — and when I tested it inwardly, I found that there was something real there.

I've experienced help. I've experienced healing. Not in dramatic, Hollywood ways — but in quiet, unmistakable ways that changed how I understand what's possible.

I'm not asking you to take my word for it. I'm just telling you what I found.

---

## 6. Closing (~1 min)

The core of Bruno Gröning's message is breathtakingly simple:

Help is available — if we learn how to receive it.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

Not that you have to earn it. Not that you have to be worthy of it. Not that you need a special teacher or a special tradition. Just that there is a goodness flowing through this world, and that when a person opens themselves to it — really opens — something can begin to change.

I think that's a message worth considering. Not as dogma. Not as religion. Just as a possibility.

And if it interests you — if something in this resonated tonight — the Circle of Friends of Bruno Gröning is active around the world, and they welcome anyone who wants to learn more.

Thank you.

## Notes for rehearsal
- The opening scene (Herford, 1949) should be delivered slowly, almost like telling a story to one person
- "I'm not asking you to believe. I'm telling you what I found." — this is the emotional center; land it
- Don't rush the Heilstrom explanation — pause after "openness" and "inner stillness"
- The personal section should feel unrehearsed, even if it isn't
- Closing: slow down. Let the last line breathe.
